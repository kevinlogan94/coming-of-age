# Game Brief: Last Week Home

## Executive Summary

*Last Week Home* is a short 2D top-down story game about Kevin’s final days in Northern Kentucky before college in 2012. Built in Phaser for a web/itch-first release, it asks the player to spend one irreplaceable week being present — walking familiar places, reading the room through texts and limited time, and sitting face-to-face with the people who made him.

The fantasy is not winning a romance or solving a mystery. It is practicing leaving well: mismatched goodbyes with a lifelong friend, parental fear dressed as discipline, and an asymmetric farewell with a girlfriend who may not meet him where he is. Choices about who you show up for shape one of three endings.

The project is an entry release — solo-led, LPC + custom/AI portraits, story and music help from trusted people — sized to prove Phaser and earn traction without a giant investment. Specificity of place, memory, and farewell structure is the product.

## Vision

**Core fantasy:** Live one last ordinary week in the place that made you — its streets, habits, and people — and practice leaving well before college takes you.

**Elevator pitch:** *Last Week Home* is a short 2D top-down story game set in Northern Kentucky, 2012. Play as Kevin in the final days before he leaves for school. Walk familiar places thick with period detail and memory, talk face-to-face with portrait conversations, and make choices that shape a handful of endings — how you say goodbye to your parents, your closest friend, and the relationship that isn’t ready for the distance.

**Emotional promise:** The player walks away feeling *this week mattered because it won’t stay — and I was there for it.* Not “I won the romance.”

**Brand image (trailer / store face):** Nick’s car drop-off — he walks away assuming continuity; Kevin already knows the chapter is ending.

**Playable emotional climax:** Rachel — asymmetric farewell (commitment poured out vs. indifference); relationship outcomes help drive endings. Betrayal/aftermath may color endings and epilogue — it is not the poster promise.

**Core message:** Everything changes — so be present with the people who love you, and make time for what matters. When life calls you forward, go. Give the world your presence.

## Target Players & Market

**Primary audience:** Players roughly 18–35 who seek short, emotionally direct narrative games (about 2–3 hours). They are time-poor but story-hungry; comfortable with choice-driven dialogue; likely to discover via shareable web/itch links, trailers, and word of mouth. Reference taste: *Life is Strange*, Persona-style social calendars (without needing the full JRPG), cozy or melancholic pixel narratives.

**Secondary audience:** Players (and some non-habitual gamers) drawn by Midwest / leaving-home nostalgia — “the week before I left” as a recognizable life moment — more than by genre fandom.

**Not the audience:** Systems-first RPG players expecting combat/builds; players hunting long 20h+ campaigns; anyone sold a betrayal-mystery or supernatural small-town thriller.

**Market posture:** Traction-friendly indie narrative lane. Price intuition for this length/production: roughly **$4.99–$9.99** if/when paid (itch free-or-PWYW demo possible first). Opportunity is cultural specificity + a clear hook (“one last week home”), not outspending the cozy-narrative crowd on production.

## Core Fundamentals

**Genre:** Short 2D top-down narrative adventure / coming-of-age story game with light social-scheduling choices.

**Playtime:** About 2–3 hours per playthrough.

**Core loop:** Enter a time block → explore a small set of places → examine for nostalgia / receive texts → choose who to show up for (talk + portrait dialogue + meaningful choices) → major scene resolves → time advances → repeat across the final week → finale and one of three endings.

**Time model:** Calendar advances after major scenes (Persona / *Life is Strange* cadence), not free-roam of the entire week at once and not sleep-only.

**Gameplay pillars:**
1. **Presence over plot** — Power comes from showing up and saying the hard thing; twist machinery is not the engine.
2. **Faces in conversation** — LPC-style top-down bodies in the world; higher-res portraits when people talk. Intimacy is a UI promise.
3. **Choices that leave a mark** — Limited social time, competing invites, and dialogue forks feed relationship/presence flags → three endings.

**Primary verbs (MVP):**
- Walk / run
- Talk (portrait dialogue + choices)
- Text (including competing hangout invites)
- Limited “who you see” per time block (choose A ⇒ you don’t see B)
- Examine (look / read / nostalgia flavor — not puzzle mini-games)

**Social-consequence pattern:** Commit to a hangout via text or plan → a second invite can arrive → accepting can mean ditching the first person → presence/relationship flags shift. Teaches *you make time for what matters* without a sermon.

**Choice → endings (model):** Track presence/honesty with **Nick**, **Rachel**, and **Family (Steve + Mom)**. Three endings reflect how Kevin leaves each thread — including space for a cleaner break with Rachel than real life, framed as honesty/presence rather than a moral high score.

**Explicitly out of MVP verb kit:** Jigsaw/puzzle genre; stealth genre (any “almost got caught” memory is a scripted story beat only, if used at all).

**Player-experience goals:** Nostalgia for a specific place and time; the ache of mismatched goodbyes; the weight of small scheduling choices; leaving forward with presence, not perfection.

## References & Differentiation

**Comparable titles — take vs. leave:**

| Title | Take | Leave |
|---|---|---|
| *Life is Strange* | Choice weight; time advancing after scenes; small-town teenage ache | Rewind powers; mystery conspiracy; long episode structure |
| *Persona 5* | Limited social time; who you see matters; competing priorities | Dungeons; combat; stylish anime JRPG scale; months-long calendar |
| *Stardew Valley* | Top-down daily life; portrait-on-talk intimacy; place attachment | Farming loop; endless seasons; marriage/meta progression |
| *The Last of Us* | Emotional seriousness; relationships as the point; quiet between storms | AAA action; gore; long cinematic campaign |

**Indirect comps (landscape, not pillars):** Short personal coming-of-age indies (*Until Then*, *Florence*, *A Space for the Unbound*, *Night in the Woods* for hometown liminality) — useful for tone/market, not feature checklists.

**Key differentiator:** A short, specific **Northern Kentucky, 2012** farewell week with autobiographical texture — LPC bodies in the world, custom higher-res dialogue portraits, text-driven social scheduling (including ditching someone), and a brand image built on a **mismatched friend goodbye** (Nick), not a supernatural mystery or a romance-betrayal poster.

**Honest edge:** Differentiation is specificity of place, memory, and farewell structure — not a novel engine tech moat. Execution and honesty of the week carry the product.

## Scope & MVP

**Team:** Solo-led by Kevin (design/dev/Phaser). Story help from Elliot. Writing help planned. Music help from brother (likely). No dedicated full-time art hire — LPC + generated/custom portraits for gaps.

**Engine / platforms:** Phaser. **Ship target:** web build on itch.io first (shareable link). Steam wrapper is a later upside, not a day-one requirement.

**MVP hypothesis to validate:** Can a short, choice-driven farewell week — portraits, texts, limited who-you-see, three endings — deliver a complete emotional experience people will finish and share?

**MVP boundary (shippable first release):**
- One compressed “final week” ≈ **8 time blocks**
- Load-bearing cast: **Kevin, Steve, Lisa (Mom), Rachel, Nick**
- Verbs: walk/run, talk+portraits, text+scheduling/ditch, examine
- **Three endings** driven by Nick / Rachel / Family presence flags
- Content pool sized so one playthrough ≈ **2–3 hours** (player sees a path through the pool; total authored scenes denser than one run)
- **Four long set-pieces:** Nick car farewell; Rachel asymmetric goodbye; Steve in-week confrontation (fear/discipline, not the later homesick texts); Mom attachment/goodbye
- Locations: small set (home, Nick drop-off route, Rachel-related place, one shared hangout) — expand only if a scene earns it
- Art: Liberated Pixel Cup (+ extensions) for world/sprites; custom or AI-assisted portraits for the load-bearing cast; keep attribution clean
- Audio: original or brother-led music bed matching melancholic nostalgia; light SFX

**Content budget (order of magnitude):** ~4 long set-pieces; ~6–8 medium scenes; ~6–10 short connective beats; explore/examine glue; opening + epilogue. Single run uses ~60–70% of the optional pool via scheduling. If content slips, **cut playtime promise** before adding filler systems.

**Out of MVP:** Allison romance branch; Kyle/Elliot quests; jigsaw; stealth; Steam-critical features; four+ endings; large open town.

## Content & Direction

**Setting:** Northern Kentucky, summer/era texture of **2012** — period phones/texts, local places, high-school aftermath (including drumline identity as backstory/examine flavor, not a full game mode). College is about an hour and a half away; the week is the threshold.

**Narrative approach:** Autofiction-flavored, grounded realism. No supernatural framing. Story told through conversations, texts, and what you miss when you choose someone else. Brand image = Nick’s mismatched farewell; climax choice cluster = Rachel; Steve’s in-week weight = fear dressed as discipline; Mom = attachment/emotional safety. The mutual homesick apology with Dad happens **after** Kevin has left — epilogue/ending color only, not a during-the-week set-piece.

**Cast (playable weight):** Steve, Mom, Rachel, Nick. Texture-only unless a scene earns later inclusion: Elizabeth, Wes, Ace; dropped from playable: Allison, Kyle, Elliot.

**Art direction:** 2D top-down LPC aesthetic in-world; **higher-res portraits** on dialogue (Stardew-like clarity vs. chunky sprites). AI-generated gap assets/portraits allowed in pipeline if curated for consistency (faces, lighting, 2012 wardrobe). Nostalgia via examineables and place dressing, not exposition dumps.

**Audio direction:** Warm, slightly melancholic underscore; space for silence in hard conversations; simple UI/text cues. Brother-led music if available.

**Replayability:** Light — alternate who you prioritize and which ending you reach. Not a content treadmill.

## Risks & Open Questions

**Risks**
- **Content volume vs. 2–3h promise** — underwriting yields a thin hour; overwriting blows solo scope. Mitigation: lock the 8-block / 4 set-piece budget; cut length before adding systems.
- **AI + LPC visual mismatch** — portraits and generated props can clash. Mitigation: strict portrait style sheet; human pick/reject; fewer faces done well.
- **LPC licensing/attribution** — many assets CC-BY-SA; keep a clean credit sheet early.
- **Autobiographical exposure** — Rachel material is potent; betrayal framing can mis-sell the game or feel unkind. Mitigation: store promise = last week home / Nick image; endings handle aftermath with care; no unconfirmed cheating as fact.
- **Phaser narrative tooling** — dialogue/state/save are build-your-own. Mitigation: JSON or Ink/Yarn-style pipeline early; prototype text+time-advance before map sprawl.
- **Crowded short-narrative market** — generic cozy pixel vibes don’t differentiate. Mitigation: NKY 2012 specificity + ditching/texts + Nick brand image.

**Open questions (for GDD)**
- Exact ending definitions (what each of the three endings shows on screen)
- Concrete location list and 2012 nostalgia reference pack
- Portrait production pipeline (Gemini prompts vs. hand touch-up standards)
- How hard ditching hits mechanically (temporary vs. ending-critical)
- Whether Ace / sister / Wes earn a single scene in a post-MVP patch
- Paid price vs. free/PWYW itch launch experiment
